1. Inventory every secret or credential type implied by the provisioning workspace, HMRC docs, and the Taxat authority boundary.
2. Normalize them into a machine-readable secret inventory with explicit classification and storage rules.
3. Define the capture-channel policy:
   manual secure entry,
   vault seeding,
   provider-portal one-time reveal capture,
   API-generated secret ingestion,
   and any prohibited channels.
4. Define the rotation matrix.

   create new,
   update dependent systems,
   verify,
   retire old,
   and audit the cutover.

   The provisioning workspace must be able to pause before a human enters a secret and continue without storing the raw secret in the repo or unsafe artifacts.
6. Define redaction rules for logs, screenshots, DOM snapshots, traces, clipboard, downloads, and browser storage.
7. Define the bootstrap-seeding contract that explains how local, CI, sandbox, and shared environments obtain only the secrets they need.

- Close the “evidence capture and browser artifacts can accidentally leak provisioning secrets” gap.
- Close the “later HMRC bootstrap cards need a vault and redaction policy before they can export credentials” gap.

Mandatory edge cases:
- A provider portal may show a secret only once; the policy must specify a safe capture and immediate vault-ingest flow.
- A browser trace or screenshot may contain a visible secret; default policy must either suppress capture at that step or redact before persistence.
- Browser `storageState` may contain authentication cookies or session tokens; if used at all, treat it as secret material with retention limits.
- A manual checkpoint may require a human to paste a code or secret; the workspace must not echo it into logs or DOM mirrors.
- Multiple client secrets may coexist temporarily during rotation; the policy must state the maximum concurrency and the cutover evidence required.
- Sandbox test-user credentials are not equivalent to Developer Hub credentials and must not share namespace or lifecycle automatically.
- Revoked or retired secret versions must remain auditable without remaining usable.
- No secret policy may imply that raw authority access or refresh tokens leave the governed vault boundary.

Architecture and security requirements:
- Store only aliases, hashes, or vault refs in repo-tracked outputs.
- Prefer a managed secret store / vault with per-environment namespaces and auditable access.
- Use encryption at rest and in transit for any secret persistence allowed outside the vault.
- Keep local developer ergonomics subordinate to security law.
  Convenience cannot justify plaintext `.env` sprawl for high-sensitivity provisioning secrets.
- Require explicit attestation or verification for secret-rotation completion.
- Distinguish between secret metadata that is safe to persist and secret values that are not.

Validation rules:
- The linter must detect missing classification, missing owner, missing vault namespace, missing rotation policy, and forbidden plaintext locations.
- The policy must explicitly state that raw secrets never appear in repo files, screenshots, traces, general logs, or CI stdout.
- Rotation steps must be machine-checkable enough that later automation can verify completion.
- The policy must align with `SecretVersion` lineage and not invent an incompatible secret lifecycle.
- Re-run the validator commands if your outputs materially affect release or security interpretation.

  with exact vault targets, human-entry rules, artifact-suppression rules, and cutover sequences already defined,
  rather than improvising secret handling mid-run.
